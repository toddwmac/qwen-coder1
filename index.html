Transformer Architecture

The transformer model, introduced in the landmark paper "Attention Is All You Need" (2017), revolutionized natural language processing by replacing recurrent and convolutional layers with self-attention mechanisms.

Core Components

Self-Attention Mechanism

The heart of the transformer. Allows each position to attend to all positions in the previous layer, computing weighted representations based on relevance.

Formula: Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ/√dₖ)V

  • Query (Q): What am I looking for?
  • Key (K): What do I contain?
  • Value (V): What information do I offer?

Multi-Head Attention

Multiple attention heads allow the model to jointly attend to information from different representation subspaces at different positions.

  • Parallel attention computations
  • Captures different types of relationships
  • Typically 8-96 heads in modern models

Positional Encoding

Since transformers have no inherent notion of sequence order, positional encodings are added to inject information about token positions.

  • Sinusoidal or learned embeddings
  • Maintains relative position information
  • Essential for understanding sequence structure

Feed-Forward Networks

Position-wise fully connected feed-forward networks apply nonlinear transformations to each position independently.

  • Two linear transformations with ReLU/GELU activation
  • Typically 4x expansion of hidden dimension
  • Processes each token independently

Layer Normalization

Normalization layers stabilize training and improve convergence. Applied before (pre-norm) or after (post-norm) attention and FFN blocks.

  • Reduces internal covariate shift
  • Enables deeper architectures
  • Pre-norm generally preferred in modern LLMs

Residual Connections

Skip connections around each sub-layer enable gradient flow through deep networks and help preserve information.

  • Add & Norm structure
  • Prevents vanishing gradients
  • Critical for training deep models

Modern Variations

Contemporary LLMs have evolved the original transformer architecture:

  • Decoder-only models: GPT series use causal masking for autoregressive generation
  • RoPE (Rotary Positional Embeddings): Better extrapolation to longer sequences
  • RMSNorm: More efficient normalization without mean centering
  • SwiGLU activations: Improved non-linearity for better performance
  • Grouped Query Attention: Reduces memory bandwidth requirements
  • Mixture of Experts (MoE): Sparse activation for efficiency at scale

LLM Training Stages

Training large language models is a complex, multi-stage process that requires careful planning, massive computational resources, and iterative refinement.

Stage 1: Pre-training

The foundation of any LLM. The model learns general language patterns, world knowledge, and reasoning capabilities from vast amounts of unlabeled text data.

📊 Data Collection

Curating diverse, high-quality datasets from web crawls, books, code repositories, scientific papers, and more. Data quality directly impacts model performance.

🔧 Tokenization

Converting text into numerical tokens using methods like BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) or SentencePiece. Typical vocabularies range from 32K to 100K+ tokens.

⚙️ Training Objective

Causal language modeling: predict the next token given all previous tokens. This autoregressive objective teaches the model language structure and coherence.

🖥️ Infrastructure

Requires thousands of GPUs/TPUs running for weeks or months. Uses techniques like gradient checkpointing, mixed precision, and pipeline parallelism.

# Simplified pre-training loop
for batch in dataloader:
    tokens = batch[:seq_len]
    labels = batch[1:seq_len+1]
    
    # Forward pass
    logits = model(tokens)
    
    # Compute cross-entropy loss
    loss = cross_entropy(logits, labels)
    
    # Backward pass & optimization
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
    optimizer.zero_grad()

Key Metrics: Training loss curves, perplexity, evaluation on benchmark datasets (MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval, etc.)

Stage 2: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Also called instruction tuning. Adapts the pre-trained model to follow instructions and engage in helpful dialogue using curated prompt-response pairs.

📝 Dataset Creation

High-quality human-written demonstrations covering diverse tasks: question answering, creative writing, coding, analysis, and more.

🎯 Format Standardization

Consistent conversation formats with clear role markers (user/assistant) and system prompts that define behavior.

📈 Training Approach

Full fine-tuning or parameter-efficient methods (LoRA, QLoRA) on 10K-1M+ examples. Lower learning rates than pre-training.

Example SFT Training Pair:

User: Explain quantum entanglement in simple terms.

Assistant: Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon where two particles become connected in such a way that measuring one instantly affects the other, regardless of distance. Imagine having two magic coins... [continues with clear, accurate explanation]

Stage 3: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

Aligns the model with human preferences by learning from comparative judgments rather than explicit correct answers.

Step 1: Preference Data Collection

Human annotators compare multiple model responses to the same prompt, ranking them by quality, helpfulness, and safety.

Step 2: Reward Model Training

Train a separate model to predict human preferences. This reward model scores responses, enabling automated optimization.

Step 3: PPO Optimization

Use Proximal Policy Optimization to update the policy (LLM) to maximize reward while staying close to the SFT model (KL penalty).

# RLHF objective function
loss = -log_prob(chosen_response) * advantage 
       + beta * kl_divergence(policy, reference_model)
       
# Maximize reward while penalizing deviation from SFT

Stage 4: Advanced Alignment & Safety

Additional refinement to ensure the model is helpful, harmless, and honest across diverse scenarios.

  • Constitutional AI: Self-critique based on written principles without human feedback at every step
  • Red Teaming: Adversarial testing to identify and fix failure modes
  • Domain-specific Fine-tuning: Specialization for medical, legal, or technical applications
  • Continuous Evaluation: Ongoing monitoring for drift, bias, and emerging risks
  • Refusal Training: Teaching appropriate boundaries for harmful requests

🔄 Iterative Process

Alignment is not a one-time event. Production models undergo continuous evaluation, feedback collection, and incremental updates to maintain quality and safety standards.

Advanced Prompting Techniques

Effective prompting unlocks the full capabilities of language models. These techniques help you get more accurate, reliable, and useful outputs.

Zero-Shot Prompting

Directly asking the model to perform a task without examples.

Example:

Classify the sentiment of this review: "The movie was fantastic!"

Few-Shot Prompting

Providing examples to demonstrate the desired format and reasoning pattern.

Example:

Q: What is 2+2? A: 4
Q: What is 3+3? A: 6
Q: What is 4+4? A: ?

Chain of Thought (CoT)

Encouraging step-by-step reasoning before providing the final answer.

Example:

Let's think step by step. First, identify the key variables. Next, determine the relevant formula...

Role Prompting

Assigning a specific persona or expertise to the model.

Example:

You are an expert physicist with 20 years of teaching experience. Explain black holes to a high school student.

Tree of Thoughts

Exploring multiple reasoning paths and selecting the best one.

Example:

Generate three different approaches to solve this problem. Evaluate each approach. Select and elaborate on the best one.

Self-Consistency

Sampling multiple outputs and selecting the most consistent answer.

Strategy:

Generate 5 independent solutions. Use majority voting or confidence scoring to select the final answer.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Combining model knowledge with external retrieved information.

Pattern:

[Retrieved context from database]

Based on the above information, answer: [user query]

Constitutional Prompting

Embedding principles and constraints directly in the prompt.

Example:

Always be truthful. If you're uncertain, say so. Never provide harmful instructions. Cite sources when possible.

Best Practices for Effective Prompting

  • Be Specific: Clearly state what you want, including format, length, and style requirements
  • Provide Context: Include relevant background information to ground the response
  • Use Delimiters: Separate instructions, context, and input with clear markers (###, """, etc.)
  • Iterate: Refine prompts based on outputs; small changes can have big impacts
  • Set Constraints: Specify what NOT to do as clearly as what TO do
  • Request Structure: Ask for tables, bullet points, or JSON when appropriate
  • Temperature Tuning: Lower temps (0.2-0.5) for factual tasks, higher (0.7-1.0) for creativity

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

❌ Vague Instructions

"Write something about AI" vs. "Write a 300-word beginner-friendly explanation of neural networks with one real-world example"

❌ Contradictory Requirements

Asking for brevity while requesting comprehensive coverage creates confusion

❌ Assuming Knowledge

The model doesn't know your specific context unless you provide it

❌ Over-complicating

Break complex tasks into multiple prompts rather than one overwhelming request
